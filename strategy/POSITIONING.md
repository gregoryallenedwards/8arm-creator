## Three belief planks

**Plank 1: The roles collapse into product engineers and workflow engineers.**

The industry splits into two kinds of people who both *decide and build*. **Product engineers** define what the product is and build the features themselves, without asking permission or waiting on a work order. **Workflow engineers** build the scaffolding that makes everyone else faster and more efficient. The middle shrinks: fewer people who only *say* what the game is, fewer people who only *take* work orders. And fewer people building the same scope of game, because small teams now cover what used to take large ones.

*Why credible:* I've shipped 50+ games. I know what overhead looks like at scale (150–300 teams). I see what AI changes: fewer approval layers, faster iteration, individuals shipping more. But that only works if the person owning it has judgment and takes responsibility for the output.

*Proof:* a002 ("Fewer managers, more doers"), plus the workflow articles (Workdeck, This 60 MCP, newsletter automation, git-repo todo list, transcript pipeline) as lived demonstration that AI amplifies individual contribution.

---

**Plank 2: The market fragments, discoverability spikes, and AI tools reach niches without traditional UA.**

More products will serve smaller niches (this follows Eric Seufert's "Prosperous Society" thesis on distribution and market fragmentation). But in the short term the discoverability problem spikes hard. The edge for a small team is AI tooling that reaches a niche audience through non-traditional means: not classic user acquisition, not classic marketing.

*Why credible:* I've shipped across 30 years of platform and distribution shifts (arcade → console → PC → mobile → live-service). I've made greenlight calls on what would sell. I've built monetization frameworks. I've read the market.

*Proof:* Discoverability analysis, Chris Zukowski market learnings, plus your own originating take on how niches and distribution align (the piece that anchors this plank).

---

**Plank 3: Vision and game thesis from day one is how you beat the distribution problem.**

AI changes speed and cost, but craft and vision matter *more*, not less. Because discoverability keeps spiking, teams must nail product-market fit from the very beginning: the vision of the game *is* the distribution strategy.

The method is concrete and proven: the **game thesis** (market statement + game statement) and the **Player Monetization Journey** (PMJ), both pointed at PMF before content is built. The game thesis specifies an unmet player need (WHO) and a game proposal to meet it (HOW). The PMJ aligns monetization with player lifecycle and retention milestones. Together they anchor the entire production pipeline.

*Why credible:* 30 years of greenlight judgment. I've set design standards, enforced feature discipline, mentored teams, greenlit projects. I know what great looks like. I've built and used the game thesis and PMJ frameworks at my last three studios. They work.

*Proof:* Match 3 white label shipped with thesis-driven production; the flagship build demonstrates the method live.
