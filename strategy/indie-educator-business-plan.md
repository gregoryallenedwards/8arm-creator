**Created:** June 9, 2026 (v2 revised same day)
**Context:** Allen is employed through approximately December 2026. **He goes on garden leave in November, which is when contracting becomes possible.** Outreach conversations are permitted starting in October. No contracts can be signed before garden leave begins. Allen's personal network is largely retired, dead, or unemployed, so this plan assumes **zero warm-network revenue**: every client must come from content, communities, or cold outreach backed by published proof. Working assumption: roughly £5,000 to £6,000 per month net required (Allen: replace with the real number, it drives every target below).

**Note on garden leave (Dutch law):** Allen's contract is governed by Netherlands employment law. The Dutch term is *vrijstelling van werk*. Key differences from UK practice: Dutch non-compete clauses (*concurrentiebeding*) must be in writing and are enforceable but can be challenged; during garden leave you remain an employee and are typically barred from competitor work, but freelance advisory in an adjacent space may be permitted depending on how the clause is worded. Confirm the exact scope with a Dutch employment lawyer (*arbeidsrechtadvocaat*) before October outreach. This check gates Phase 3. Currency throughout this plan is in euros unless otherwise noted.

## 1. Verdict

The idea is viable, but not as a pure media business. Content alone takes 2 to 4 years to reach family-supporting income. The model that works on this timeline: **content is the marketing department for a B2B advisory and training business.** Because contracting isn't possible until garden leave in November, the constraint actually dictates the right sequence: spend June through October building the audience, the proof library, and the waitlist; open the books when garden leave starts.

## 2. Allen's Lane: Three Stacked Differentiators

**The framing risk first:** "Indie dev success educator" invites *"you've never shipped a successful indie game."* Don't fight it; reframe around what nobody else has.

### Differentiator 1: What makes a good game (the Zukowski gap)
Zukowski tells devs how to market a game and openly says he can't tell them whether the game itself is good or how to make it good. That is exactly Allen's 30 years: greenlight criteria, core loop evaluation, hook clarity, scope versus team capability, genre convention literacy, monetization fit. **This is the content's center of gravity, not a side dish.** The existing PowerPoints on focusing game development are a pre-built curriculum for it.

### Differentiator 2: The publisher's chair
Allen greenlit and killed games, secured a $60M budget, ran P&L on a Top 30 Steam publisher portfolio. He can show devs the inside of the meeting that decides their fate. Positioning line: *"I spent 30 years deciding which games get funded and why they make money. I'll show you what the people writing the checks are looking for, and how to make a game that passes."*

### Differentiator 3: AI builder who runs experiments and shows results
Allen builds with AI tools and can run cheap, fast, public experiments: prototype a mechanic in a day, A/B test hooks, fake-door test a concept, then publish the numbers. None of the incumbents build anything in public. This turns claims into demonstrations: *"I don't just tell you this hook is weak. I built three versions and tested them. Here's the data."* (Components of the validation engine only; the full engine stays a crown jewel per content-creator-rules.md.)

**Combined position: the only voice in indie games that can judge a game like a publisher, teach what makes it good like a director, and prove it with AI-built experiments like a builder.**

## 3. The Content: Four Explicit Pillars

This is what the Substack and YouTube actually are. Every piece of content belongs to one pillar.

### Pillar A: "What Makes a Good Game" (craft and greenlight criteria)
The teaching spine, drawn from the PowerPoints and 30 years of greenlight meetings.
- Example posts/episodes: "The 5 questions every greenlight meeting asks (and how 90% of pitches fail question 2)" · "Hook versus loop: why your trailer views don't convert" · "Scope math: how a publisher estimates whether your team can actually ship this" · "Genre conventions you can break and the ones that kill you"
- This pillar becomes the course curriculum (Section 5, Layer 3).

### Pillar B: "Greenlight or Pass" (applied analysis, the flagship show)
Real games and submitted pitches reviewed exactly like a publisher greenlight meeting: market, hook, loop quality, scope versus team, monetization, verdict.
- Sources: recent indie launches and postmortems, plus a **free pitch-submission form** (devs submit decks for public review, anonymized on request). Every submission is an episode AND a future-client lead.
- Example episodes: "This game made £2M and I'd have passed on it. Both facts are true, here's why" · "Live greenlight: a submitted co-op roguelike, verdict at the end"

### Pillar C: AI Builder Experiments (proof in public)
Build, test, publish numbers. Ties directly to the existing PROJECTS.md backlog (Match 3 Test, Fishing Game, fake door tests, sentiment crawling components).
- Example posts/episodes: "I built a match-3 hook 3 ways with Claude in one weekend and fake-door tested all three. Results inside" · "Can AI playtest? I ran an agent through my prototype 50 times" · "From idea to testable web prototype in 6 hours: the full pipeline" · "I ran my own game through my own greenlight framework on camera. It failed."
- This pillar also quietly markets the advisory work to funded studios: it proves the methods are operational, not theoretical.

### Pillar D: Inside the Publisher (career authority, light cadence)
Occasional pieces that establish who Allen is: how greenlight portfolios actually work, how monetization gets designed, what a $60M pitch looks like from the inside. No My.Games specifics, partners, or competitors until after exit.

**Cadence:** 1 flagship piece per week (Pillar A or B), 1 to 2 per month from Pillar C (experiments take longer), Pillar D occasionally. Substack is the written home and email list; YouTube is the discovery spearhead once launched; LinkedIn carries the key insight from each piece. Podcast: skip (worst discovery, worst monetization, highest production tax).

## 4. Client Acquisition With No Warm Network

Assume nobody Allen already knows will buy. Clients come from four machines, all of which run on published proof:

1. **The pitch-submission funnel.** Free public reviews attract exactly the people who later pay for private ones. The form asks one qualifying question ("Are you funded, seeking funding, or hobbyist?"), which sorts leads automatically. Funded and seeking-funding submitters are the future client list.
2. **Community presence (the existing Reddit model: don't make a statement, send a free product).** r/gamedev, r/IndieDev, r/gamedesign, r/playmygame, How To Market A Game community, indie Discords, TIGSource. Drop free artifacts: the Greenlight Checklist, free review threads ("I greenlit games for 30 years, drop your pitch, I'll give verdicts"), experiment results. Never pitch services in-thread; the profile and Substack do that silently.
3. **Cold outreach with proof (October onward, see Phase 2).** Targets are *strangers*, selected by signal, not acquaintance: studios announcing funding or publisher signings (trackable via GameDiscoverCo, press, Steam upcoming lists), small publishers, NL/EU/UK game funds and accelerators. The opener is a free, specific artifact: a one-page greenlight read on *their* title. This is the Reddit model applied to B2B.
4. **Events (Amsterdam/EU advantage).** Develop:Brighton (July), Gamescom (August, Cologne — short trip from Amsterdam), Dutch Game Awards, A MAZE Amsterdam, Casual Connect. While employed, attend as a content creator gathering material and meeting devs; in October, the same rooms become outreach venues.

The asset that replaces a network is the **proof library**: by October 1 there must be 14+ published teardowns, 2+ experiment write-ups, and 2 to 3 unsolicited "spec" greenlight audits of well-known indie games that demonstrate the paid deliverable's quality.

Funded studios, publishers, and investors are the customers; solo indies are the audience and funnel.

### Layer 2: Productized reviews (November onward, sold from content)
Pricing page goes live November 1. Until then, every piece of content builds the **waitlist**: "Advisory books open November 1, join the list."

### Layer 3: Training (bigger pillar than v1, given Pillar A)
- **"What Makes a Good Game" / "Get Greenlit" cohort course:** 4 weeks, £500 to £800 per seat, 10 to 20 seats (£5k to £15k per run). Built from the PowerPoints plus audience questions. First cohort February/March 2027, presold to the email list; 8-seat minimum or refund and delay.
- A free email mini-course (5 lessons from Pillar A) as the mid-funnel lead magnet between checklist and cohort.
- Later: self-serve course, paid Substack tier, community, workshops for publishers/accelerators (B2B training pays best: one accelerator workshop can match a cohort).

### Financial reality
- Content-only path to £6k/month: 2 to 3 years. Not survivable alone.
- Advisory path: two fractional clients or equivalent per month. With no warm network this realistically takes longer than v1 assumed: **£2 to 3k/month by end of Q1 2027, £4 to 6k/month by end of Q3 2027** are honest targets. The gap is covered by severance, savings, and inbound fractional/contract work (which is the same offer as the business).

## 6. Phased Blueprint (re-dated for the October constraint)

### Phase 0: Foundation (June 2026, weeks 1 to 3)
1. **Compliance rules in writing.** No seeking/signing contracts before end of October. No My.Games portfolio, partner, or competitor commentary until exit. Personal time and equipment only. Publishing analysis content under his own name: confirm contract permits it (this is the one remaining legal check).
2. **Niche statement.** Primary audience: premium PC/console indie devs. Primary future customers: funded studios, small publishers, investors. Mobile F2P: consulting-only sideline later.
3. **Asset audit.** Sort the PowerPoints and frameworks into: lead magnet material, Pillar A episode list, course skeleton, consulting deliverable templates, crown jewels (never public). First artifact: the one-page **Publisher Greenlight Checklist**, gated on Substack signup.
4. **Infrastructure.** Pitch-submission form (with the funded/seeking/hobbyist question), Substack section structure for the four pillars, content calendar 8 weeks ahead. Business registration (Netherlands: ZZP *eenmanszaak* via KVK for the simplest freelance structure, or BV if you want liability separation — discuss with a Dutch accountant), Stripe, Calendly: prepare in September, activate when garden leave begins.

### Phase 1: Build audience and proof library (June to September 2026). NO SELLING.
1. **Weekly flagship post** (Pillar A and B alternating) on Substack, key insight cross-posted to LinkedIn. 14+ posts banked/published by October 1.
2. **First two AI experiments published** (Pillar C). Candidate one: take Match 3 Test from the backlog, build it in public, run it through the greenlight framework, publish the verdict and the data.
3. **Open pitch submissions** for free public review (free content, not contract work, so it is allowed now). Target: 20+ submissions by October 1.
4. **Community seeding** per Section 4.2: one free-product drop per month into a relevant community.
5. **Launch YouTube in September** with 3 episodes ready (Greenlight or Pass format), once 10+ written pieces exist to convert and the format is proven in writing first.
6. **Develop:Brighton (July) and/or Gamescom (August):** attend, meet devs, gather episode material, collect emails. No business solicitation.
7. **September 1 validation gate (audience-based, since money is off-limits):** at least one of 500+ Substack subscribers, 20+ pitch submissions, or 3 community drops that each drove 50+ signups. If none, spend two weeks repositioning (most likely harder toward Pillar A teaching content) before continuing.

### Phase 2: October 2026, the outreach month. CONVERSATIONS, NOT CONTRACTS.
The bridge month: permitted to reach out, not yet permitted to sign.
1. **Build the target list (week 1):** 50 cold-but-signaled targets: studios with recent funding/publisher announcements (15 to 20), small publishers (10), EU/NL/UK game funds and accelerators (10), platform and ecosystem people (10). Sources: GameDiscoverCo, GamesIndustry.biz funding news, Steam upcoming, accelerator cohort pages. Amsterdam and the broader Benelux/DACH indie scene is a geographic edge: you are local to studios others cold-call from overseas.
2. **Outreach rhythm: 10 per week, 40+ total.** The opener is a gift, not a pitch: a one-page greenlight read on their announced title, or the relevant published teardown. Ask: 20-minute conversation about their pitch/portfolio process.
3. **The close of every conversation:** "My advisory books open November 1. Want me to hold a slot?" Goal: **15 conversations, 8 to 10 waitlist commitments.**
4. **Publish the pricing page in draft** ("books open Nov 1") mid-October; announce to the email list and in every piece of content.
5. **Event presence:** Dutch Game Awards and any October Amsterdam/EU events, now as outreach venues.
6. **Prepare the paperwork:** KVK registration active, Stripe live, engagement letter template ready, so contracts can sign the day garden leave begins.

### Phase 3: Open for business (garden leave begins, November 2026)
1. **Day 1 of garden leave: convert the waitlist.** Founding-client framing: first 5 clients get 20% off in exchange for a testimonial and case-study rights. Have engagement letters pre-drafted so you can sign same-day.
2. Continue weekly content and 10 outreaches/week; outreach is now allowed to close.
3. **December 31 targets:** 1,500 to 3,000 Substack subscribers · 1,000+ YouTube subscribers · 2 to 4 paid engagements, £2,000 to £8,000 banked · 3 testimonials · 5+ qualified pipeline.
4. **December decision gate (job ends):** full commit in January if pipeline credibly supports £2 to 3k/month by end of Q1 AND severance + savings cover 6+ months at zero revenue. Otherwise: pursue fractional/contract work as primary income (it is the same offer as the business) and run the business at half throttle. Throttle decision, not a kill decision.

### Phase 4: Full-time ramp (January to June 2027)
1. Revenue: £2 to 3k/month by end of Q1, £4 to 6k/month by end of Q3. Mix target: 60% advisory/fractional, 25% training, 15% productized reviews and paid subs.
2. Sales rhythm forever: 10 outreach/follow-up conversations per week. Content warms them; you still have to ask.
3. **First cohort course February/March** (presold, 8-seat minimum). One run per quarter. Pitch accelerator/publisher workshops in parallel (B2B training).
4. Double content output (2 flagship pieces/week or 1 video + 1 premium written teardown behind a paid tier).
5. One AI-built game per quarter, in public, through the greenlight framework on camera (Pillar C continues; credibility engine, never the revenue plan).

### Phase 5: Scale (H2 2027 onward)
Self-serve course, paid community, annual "State of Indie Greenlight" report (sponsorable data product), publisher due diligence retainers, finder fees. Hire an editor at ~£8k/month revenue.
